Brad Cannell, PhD, MPH is the co-founder and owner of Epi-Workbench, LLC.

As a child, Brad Cannell was incredibly close to his great-grandmother. They spent afternoons watching St. Louis Cardinals games together, and he admired her deeply. While Brad was in high school, she developed dementia and was eventually moved to a nursing home. Her health declined, and she passed away in 2005 while Brad was serving overseas in the U.S. Army. Though he was unable to attend her funeral, her life, her battle with dementia, and her death became the inspiration for his career. Upon returning to the United States, he dedicated himself to researching aging, improving late-life quality of life, and preventing elder mistreatment.

Dr. Cannell earned his PhD in Epidemiology and a Graduate Certificate in Gerontology from the University of Florida in 2013. He received his MPH with a concentration in Epidemiology from the University of Louisville in 2009, and his BA in Political Science and Marketing from the University of North Texas in 2005. During his doctoral studies, he was a Graduate Research Assistant for the Florida Office on Disability and Health, an affiliated scholar with the Claude D. Pepper Older Americans Independence Center, and a student-inducted member of the Delta Omega Honorary Society in Public Health. In 2016, Dr. Cannell received a Graduate Certificate in Predictive Analytics from the University of Maryland University College, and a Certificate in Big Data and Social Analytics from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. 

Throughout his career, Dr. Cannell has held various public health and leadership roles across government, academia, and the military. He worked in the Louisville Metro Health Department and the Northern Kentucky Independent District Health Department before serving as a project epidemiologist for the Florida Office on Disability and Health at the University of Florida. From 2009 to 2013, he also served as an Environmental Science Officer in the United States Army Reserves. Currently, he is a member of the National Collaboratory to Address Elder Mistreatment and serves on the Tarrant County Adult Protective Services Community Board.

Dr. Cannell’s research focuses on healthy aging and late-life quality of life, with specific expertise in preserving physical and cognitive function, aging with disability, and detecting and preventing elder mistreatment. He has a strong background in epidemiologic methods and predictive analytics, which he applies to advance research in these areas. His work has resulted in more than 50 peer-reviewed publications, two textbook chapters, and one textbook, with papers appearing in top-tier journals such as JAMA Network Open, The American Journal of Epidemiology, and The Journal of the American Geriatrics Society.

Dr. Cannell has secured approximately $30 million in research funding through a diverse range of federal agencies, including the National Institutes of Health (NIH), the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ), the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), and the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs (VA). He has served as principal investigator on multiple federally funded projects, leading research aimed at improving aging-related health outcomes. Currently, he directs the Detection of Elder abuse Through Emergency Care Technicians (DETECT) project, which has screened more than 90,000 older adults for mistreatment and connected them with necessary services.

In addition to his research, Dr. Cannell is an experienced educator and mentor. He has developed and taught eight different courses at three universities, focusing on epidemiologic methods, data science, and public health. He is committed to training the next generation of researchers and clinicians, regularly mentoring students and early-career investigators in aging research, epidemiology, and grant writing. His dedication to education extends beyond the classroom, as he frequently delivers invited lectures and workshops on topics related to aging, public health, and research methodology.

His contributions to aging research have been recognized with numerous awards, including selection for the National Institute on Aging’s Butler-Williams Scholar Program, the Leighton E. Cluff Aging Research Award from the University of Florida, the Leadership in Integrated Healthcare Award from The Academy of International Mobile Healthcare Integration, and the R. Palmer Beasley Faculty Award for Innovation from the University of Texas Health Science Center at Houston.
