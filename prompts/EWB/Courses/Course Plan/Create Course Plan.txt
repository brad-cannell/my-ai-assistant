# Background

I teach a university course title, "Introduction to R Programming and Data Management." The course aims to provide students with hands-on experience in R programming and data management. The students should be familiar with basic concepts in epidemiology and biostatistics. Previous experience in using SAS, Stata or SPSS is helpful, but not required. Topics include downloading and installing R, basic programming concepts, basic programming best practices, R packages and environments, R data structures, data transfer, creating and manipulating data, visualizing data, conditional operations, working with multiple data frames, restructuring data frames, repeated/iterative operations, writing functions, basic analyses used in epidemiology, and techniques for presenting results to various audiences. Special emphasis will be given to using the Tidyverse family of R packages.

The primary hub for the course is the [Canvas Learning Management System](https://www.instructure.com/canvas).

That course uses the R for Epidemiology (R4Epi) textbook, which is freely available online at https://www.r4epi.com/, supplementary readings, and the following 5 types of assignments.

1. Readings (0% of final grade): These are the primary mode of content delivery. They should be fun. If they aren’t fun, I want students to let me know so that I can try to make them fun.

2. Check on learning (COL) quizzes (30% of final grade): Students complete a short reading comprehension quiz in each learning module. The goal of COL quizzes is simply to assess basic comprehension of the assigned materials and provide students with feedback early in the module when the stakes are low. Students may complete COL quizzes as many times as they like. Only the highest score counts.

3. Lab assignments (30% of final grade): Each module will include a lab assignment. They are an opportunity for students to practice applying that module’s concepts with the help of the instructor (via written feedback). Students may complete lab assignments as many times as they like. Only the highest score counts.

4. Weekly module quizzes (40% of final grade): Each module will include a quiz to be completed independently on Canvas. These quizzes provide an opportunity for students to self-assess their progress while working independently. They also provide the instructor with an opportunity to assess each student's progress.

For some EWB course, particularly the more basic ones, we may want to combine multiple R4Epi chapters together.

Navigating the RStudio Interface". My plan is also to create a course -- possibly called "Getting Started" -- that teaches learners how to use the EWB platform. In that course, we will also acknowledge that there is a tradeoff for the convenience that comes with the EWB platform -- learners don't get practice installing R, installing and interacting with an IDE (like RStudio), and installing R packages. We may also provide them with links to the R4Epi chapters above as a reference for doing so, but not as part of an EWB course.

However, I don't want to worry about the EWB "Getting Started" course yet. I want to do that later after we've completed several substantive courses and have a better understanding of what learners need to know to successfully use the EWB platform. At this point, I don't feel like I know what needs to be included in the Getting Started course because we don't have any complete courses on the EWB platform and the platform itself is still in development and undergoing minor changes.
