In this chat, I would like your help with creating content for Module 4: The DETECT-RPC Tool and REDCap/EMR Use). The learning objectives for this module are:

- Demonstrate the ability to use the DETECT-RPC screening tool within existing clinical workflows.
	
	- Successfully navigate and utilize the DETECT-RPC tool in REDCap/EMR systems.
	
	- Accurately complete screening items and provide meaningful responses to open-ended fields in the tool.

To accomplish these learning objectives, we will create four short videos that walk clinicians through using the DETECT-RPC screening tool. These videos will consist primarily of screenshots of the DETECT-RPC screening tool that are accompanied by a narrator describing the actions the clinician should take to complete the screening process. The total length of the module, including all videos below, should be 8 minutes or less. 

Here is a draft outline for the four videos:

- Video 1: Re-introduction (30 – 60 seconds)

	- Briefly remind clinicians about the core elements of the study, that they were randomized to the experimental group, and that they will be given access to the DETECT-RPC screening tool.

- Video 2: Accessing The DETECT-RPC Screening Tool (Approximately 1 minute)

	- How to locate the DETECT-RPC tool within REDCap or the EMR.

- Video 3: Completing The DETECT-RPC Screening Tool (Approximately 4 minute)

	- Section 1: Submission Details

	- Section 2: Indicators of Mistreatment Module

		- This section contains a checklist of indicators of potential EM. They are further separated into environmental indicators, indicators related to the caregiver, and patient-related indicators.	

	- Section 3: EM Status Impression

		- The clinician will use their systematic observations of the patient and the patient’s physical and social environment, and their clinical judgment to form an impression about whether the patient is potentially living with elder mistreatment.

	- Section 4: Response

		- The clinician will indicate their intention to refer for services for the patient including APS. The linked reporting guidance cheat sheets may be helpful.

	- Section 5: Reflection (optional)

		- Clinicians may optionally use this section to alert the study team to a patient who may have a particularly interesting or impactful story.

- Video 4: Completing the Process (Approximately 1 minute)

	- Contacting APS and letting your site champion know about it.

Slide 4

As discussed in the Welcome video, one of the goals of the DETECT-RPC project is to inform the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force's recommendations on screening for elder mistreatment in primary care settings. Specifically screening ALL older adults in primary care settings. Additionally, as you know, older adults’ condition can change rapidly. Elder mistreatment screening should be conducted at every patient encounter. This ensures that we do not miss cases and allows DETECT-RPC to inform the USPSTF’s recommendations for universal screening.

So providers prefer to complete it at the beginning of the encounter and others like to complete it near the end. However, we ask that you complete while you are with the patient or immediately after leaving the patinet’s home so that your observations are as accurate as possible.

You can click on the link below this video to access a REDCap version of the DETECT-RPC screening tool that has been set up in a test environment. We encourage you to explore the test environment. Practicing now will help you feel confident using DETECT-RPC in real patient encounters.

# Video 3

Here is a draft script for video 3. Please offer suggestions for improving it.

Slide 1

Welcome to video 3: Completing The DETECT-RPC Screening Tool. In this video, we will walk through completing a elder mistreatment screening using the DETECT-RPC screening tool step by step.


Slide 2

Once you've accessed the tool, you will see the DETECT-RPC screening interface. The tool is designed to be user-friendly, with clearly labeled sections guiding you through each step. The first section of the tool labeled submission details, is designed to capture a minimal amount of essential administrative data. In this section, you will type your patient's MRN into the text box and select your name and institution from the provided list. This will ensure that the screening is linked to the correct medical encounter in your institution's EMR data. Please be sure to double-check this information for accuracy.

Slide 3

The next three sections of the DETECT-RPC screening tool are collectively called the indicators of elder mistreatment module. This module will help you systematically assess "potential" elder mistreatment through observable environmental, caregiver, and patient-related indicators. In these sections, you will document signs of potential EM based on your observations and clinical judgment. Although, this tool is based primarily on your observations, you may also consider relevant information from patient conversations. To complete the indicators of abuse module, you will select "Yes," "No," or “Unable to assess" for each indicator.

Slide 4

For example, you will complete two observational items about the patient’s physical environment. The first item asks if your patient is currently living with an absence of necessities such as food and essential utilities. You will simply select "Yes", "No", or "Unable to assess" based on your observations of the current state of your patient’s environment and your clinical judgment. So, if YOU believe your patient lacks necessities, then select “Yes.” It doesn't matter if you formed that belief because the patient told you they lack necessities or if you formed it for some other reason – perhaps something you observed. Both are equally valid reasons to select “Yes.” Conversely, if you don’t believe they lack necessities, for whatever reason, then you should select “No.” If you are genuinely unable to determine if they lack necessities, you may select the “Unable to assess” option.

Slide 5

If you select the “Unable to Assess” option, an optional textbox will appear that will ask for more information. The purpose of asking you for more information isn't to second-guess your judgment. It is to better understand the barriers you face in home-based primary care, and to improve the DETECT program in the future.

Slide 6

To complete the indicators of elder mistreatment module, you will respond to the eight remaining observational items in exactly the same way.

Slide 7

After you select a response for the nine observational items in the indicators of elder mistreatment module, you will be asked if you suspect that the patient is experiencing elder mistreatment. 

As a reminder, you don’t have to be 100% sure your patient is experiencing elder mistreatment to select “Yes.” Additionally, you don’t have to be able to identify who is responsible for the mistreatment. In fact, in many states, self-neglect is the most commonly reported form of elder mistreatment. To select “Yes,” you need only suspect that your patient is potentially living with elder mistreatment and that they may benefit from services.

For example, if your patient appears malnourished but you’re unsure if it’s due to neglect, it’s still appropriate to mark ‘suspected EM’ so the case can be further evaluated.

Your suspicion, or lack of suspicion, should be based on a combination of your observations and your expert clinical judgment. In other words, you may suspect that your patient is currently living with elder mistreatment even if you selected “No” for all 9 observational items in the indicators of elder mistreatment module. Similarly, you may not suspect that your patient is currently living with elder mistreatment even if you selected “Yes” to one or more of the 9 observational items in the indicators of elder mistreatment module.

Slide 8

If you select “No” for all nine observational items in the indicators of elder mistreatment module, and you select “No” for the EM status impression item as shown here, then the only thing you have left to do is click the submit button. Once you do, the screening is complete.

Slide 9

However, if you select “Yes” for any of the nine observational items in the indicators of elder mistreatment module and you select “No” for the EM status impression item, an optional textbox will appear, as shown here. The purpose of the textbox is to gather information about why you don’t suspect potential elder mistreatment even though you observed one or more of the indicators of potential EM above. 

Again, the purpose of asking you for more information isn't to second-guess your judgment. By virtue of participating in this research, you are helping to develop and test the DETECT-RPC tool. Completing this text box isn’t required, but adding a brief explanation for your response pattern will help us to make the tool and screening process better and more efficient in the future. Some changes may even be implemented before the end of the study.

And again, once you click the submit button, the screening is complete.

Slide 10

If you select “Yes” for any of the nine observational items in the indicators of elder mistreatment module, and you select “Yes” for the EM status impression item as shown here, then you will be asked to choose the type, or types, of mistreatment you suspect. You may choose more than one type. You may also choose "other" to specify a type that wasn't listed. 

If you are unsure what constitutes the different types of mistreatment, you may click the “Definitions for EM types” link.

Slide 11

If you do, you will be taken to the National Center on Elder Abuse’s webpage about types of elder mistreatment. This website gives a definition for and examples of each type of mistreatment. 

As a side note, this link doesn’t take you to the live version of the website. Instead, it takes you to a cached, or saved, version of what the website looked like when this training was created. As a result, it may take a few moments longer to load than you are used to, and it may have a funny-looking timeline displayed at the top of the screen. 

We chose to use this cached version of the website in case the live website is ever taken down, and to ensure the definitions we gave you were consistent over the entire length of the study.

Slide 12

If you view the EM type definitions and you are still genuinely unsure what type you suspect, you may choose the ”Don’t know/ Not sure” option.

Slide 13

If you don’t select “Yes” for any of the nine observational items in the indicators of elder mistreatment module, but you do select “yes” for the EM status impression item, an optional textbox will appear, as shown here. The purpose of the textbox is to gather information about why you suspect potential elder mistreatment even though you did not observe any of the indicators of potential EM above. 

Again, the purpose of asking you for more information isn't to second-guess your judgment, and completing this text box isn’t required, but adding a brief explanation for your response pattern will help us to make the tool and screening process better and more efficient in the future.

You will still select the types of mistreatment suspect in the same way we just discussed.

Slide 14

The last element of the EM Status Impression section is a link to the reporting guidance cheat sheets you saw in one of the previous training modules about mandated reporting laws.

This link doesn’t exist in the test environment version of the DETECT-RPC screening tool linked below this video because the cheat sheets are states-specific. However, we once agaoin provide separate links to those cheat sheets in the page below this video.

Slide 15

If you select “Yes” for the EM status impression item – that you suspect that your patient is potentially living with EM -- you will be prompted to answer two questions about your intention to refer your patient for services, including APS.

Slide 16

The first question will ask you if you intend to contact APS and report suspected potential EM. This question is not asking if you already contacted APS. It is simply asking of you intend to contact APS. If you do, please select “Yes.” 

Slide 17

After indicating that you intend to contact APS and report suspected potential EM, a message will appear on the screen reminding you to contact your site champion and document your APS report after you make it. Documenting your APS reports is important because it is one of the primary study outcomes. 

As a side note, one or two study sites use a different process for documenting APS reports. For example, contacting a social worker instead of the site champion. If you are providing care at one of those sites, please think of this message as a reminder to follow that process instead.

Slide 18

Alternatively, if you indicate that you do not intend to contact APS here after previously indicating that you suspect potential EM, an optional textbox will appear requesting a brief explanation. Once again, the purpose of this textbox is not to question your judgment. It is to help us improve the tool and screening process.

Slide 19

The second question in the Response section will ask you if you intend to refer your patient and/or their caregiver for any other services. If you do, please select “Yes.” When you select “Yes,” an optional textbox will appear requesting a brief explanation of the services you intended to refer to. For example, social work services provided by your institution. meals on wheels, or law enforcement. 


Slide 20

If you don’t intend to to refer your patient and/or their caregiver for any other services, please select “No.” 

Slide 21

The final section in DETECT-RPC is the Reflection and Notes section, and it is completely optional. The quantitative data you are helping us collect through your participation in this study will be incredibly valuable. However, quantitative data only tells part of the story. Over the years, we have found that stories from and about our participants provide an additional rich and valuable source of information we can learn from. Therefore, please check “Yes” to this question if you believe that your patient or their caregiver may be able to provide us with a story that helps us understand the DETECT-RPC screening process, elder mistreatment, or anything else that may be useful to the project.

If you do, an optional textbox will appear. You may add as many notes as you’d like about why you think this patient’s story could be useful – including none at all. We will follow up with you later for more details if we need to.

Slide 22

Alternatively, you may select “No” if you aren’t aware of any story about this patient that seems like it would be relevant or useful to the study.

Slide 23

When you have completed all of the required items, and as many of the optional items as you care to, then the only thing you have left to do is click the submit button. Once you do, the screening is complete.

Because we are walking through every possible branch of the screening process, this training video might give you the impression that the screening process is long and laborious. However, in our pilot tests with other clinicians like yourselves – including your site champions – the overwhelming sentiment was that the tool was quick and easy to use.

Here are actual comments from clinicians who pilot tested the tool:

“I found it really easy to use. I do not see this being cumbersome to use in any way.”

“Tool is quick and easy to use, this was in no way a burden or time-consuming.”

Slide 24

After you click the submit button, you will see a screen thanking you for completing the screening. 

There is also a link on this screen that you can use to complete another screening. Please use this link to create another screening instead of clicking your browser’s back button. If you use the back button, it creates errors in the database.

Slide 25

Currently, it isn’t possible for you to directly edit a DETECT-RPC screening after you’ve submitted it. If you would like to modify a screening that you’ve already submitted, it’s best to create and complete a new screening. After you do, please email your site champion or the study team to let them know. One of the database administrators will remove the original screening from the database.

Slide 1

In this video, we will briefly discuss how to report potential elder mistreatment to Adult Protective Services or APS.

Slide 2

Whenever you suspect an older adult is potentially experiencing elder mistreatment, your first call should be to Adult Protective Services or APS. APS systems and contact information vary from state to state, so we can’t list a single website or phone number you can use to contact APS. However, the first or second result from a quick Google search should generally contain contact information for APS in your state. 

However, to make things even easier, we’ve provided a QR code, clickable link, and phone number for APS in your state on the state-specific reporting guidance cheat sheets you’ve seen multiple times now. For example, here is a screenshot from the cheat sheet for Alabama.

Slide 3

# Video of Ron saying to have the older adult call with you and call it a consultation.

Slide 4

The reporting forms can sometimes seem long and daunting. However, you don’t need to complete every single section of the reporting form to initiate an APS response. In general, APS needs you to report some basic information, including:
Your name and contact information.

Slide 5

# Video of Laura saying she gives APS her cell phone number.

Slide 6

In addition to your contact information, here we can see other essential and non-essential but helpful information to include in your report.

The Name, age, and address of the older adult.

A brief description of the situation that led you to suspect potential EM.

If applicable, the name of the person or people engaging in abusive or neglectful behaviors.

And any other helpful details. For example, current injuries, medical problems, or behavioral problems.

Slide 7

After making a report, here is what you can expect to happen.
APS will assess the immediate risk to the older adult.
If you made a report online, they may attempt to contact you for more information. While their role is investigative, your insights as a clinician are crucial in ensuring the patient receives appropriate care.
They will attempt to visit the older adult in-person.
They will assess the older adult’s capacity to make informed decisions, including whether to cooperate with the APS investigation.
If they do cooperate, then APS will develop a case plan.
This can include providing short-term case management & counseling and arranging for the provision of supportive services.
Additionally, APS can often assist with health and/or financial benefits applications.
Finally, in extreme cases, APS may apply for temporary emergency guardianship and testify in court actions

Slide 8

In summary, we all have an ethical and legal obligation to report potential elder mistreatment. You don’t have to be certain, a suspicion is enough. And remember, it isn’t your responsibility to fully investigate or remedy a situation where mistreatment is occurring. That is APS’s job.
