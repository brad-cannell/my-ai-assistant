I am an academic researcher who is the principal investigator of a large study that is funded by the National Institute on Aging (NIA).

The name of the study is “Detection of Elder abuse Through Emergency Care Technicians – Revised for Primary Care” or “DETECT-RPC” for short. We previously developed and tested an elder mistreatment (EM) screening tool designed specifically for use by EMTs and paramedics in older adults’ homes. The project and screening tool was called Detection of Elder abuse Through Emergency Care Technicians (DETECT). The DETECT tool was designed to (1) be brief, (2) be based on the clinician’s direct observations of the older adult and the older adult’s physical and social environment, (3) provide reporting guidance, and (4) be integrated into their existing procedures and medical charting software.

Our current objective is to adapt the DETECT screening tool for home-based primary care (HBPC) — with special emphasis on older adults who were recently diagnosed with Alzheimer’s Disease and Related Dementias (ADRD) — DETECT Revised for Primary Care (DETECT-RPC). Additionally, we believe that the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) and the Abuse Intervention Model (AIM) are two theories that will help explain why the revised DETECT-RPC screening tool changes, or does not change, home-based primary care clinician APS reporting behavior.

We have completed the following steps towards adapting DETECT for home-based primary care:

1. We completed focus groups with 12 home-based primary care clinicians about barriers and facilitators to recognizing and reporting EM during medical encounters.

2. We created an initial draft of the screening items we plan to use on the DETECT-RPC screening tool.

3. We completed an intervention mapping process designed to help plan our intervention. Specifically, it maps together the underlying theories – TPB and AIM – that we believe help explain home-based primary care clinician reporting behavior, the barriers and facilitators reported by the focus group participants, and the DETECT-RPC screening items and procedures.

Now, we are creating a training program for HBPC clinicians to complete before using the DETECT-RPC screening tool with their patients. 

The attached Word document titled "Experimental Group Training Outline.docx" contains an overview of the training program, the training program's learning objectives, some details about the individual training modules that make up the training program, and some development notes.

For each of the following pieces of training content, we want to create recorded interviews with experts in the field:

1. A video featuring a geriatrician discussing their experiences screening patients for EM. This is not intended to be a "how-to" video describing techniques for conducting such a screening; rather, it is a video about the geriatrician's subject experience with how these screenings feel in the moment and how they impact their relationship with their patients. This video aims to address HBPC clinicians' fears that screening for and reporting EM will be difficult and unpleasant.

2. A video featuring a geriatrician discussing how to preserve therapeutic relationships with patients after determining they need to contact Adult Protective Services post-screening to report potential EM. This video aims to address HBPC clinicians' fears that screening for and reporting EM will harm their therapeutic relationships with their patients.

3. A video featuring an expert teaching HBPC clinicians how to have discussions with their patients about contacting Adult Protective Services (APS) post-screening to report potential EM. This video is intended to be a "how-to" video describing techniques the HBPC clinicians can use to facilitate these discussions. Many of the HBPC clinicians we interviewed in our preliminary focus groups expressed apprehension about these conversations with patients and caregivers. They expressed feeling as though they lacked the knowledge, experience, and practical skills needed to navigate such a difficult conversation topic successfully.

4. A video featuring an APS professional discussing what APS does and doesn't do after the HBPC clinician reports potential EM. This video aims to address gaps in HBPC clinicians' understanding of APS's mission (i.e., social services, not law enforcement) and its investigation process. This video should also discuss differences in the APS response for patients who are living with cognitive impairment or who are undocumented immigrants. This video aims to address HBPC clinicians' fears about what happens after they file an APS report. For example, many of the HBPC clinicians we interviewed in our preliminary focus groups expressed concern that APS would remove their patient from the home or arrest the caregiver.

5. A video featuring an APS professional discussing the need for more reports. This video aims to address HBPC clinicians' fears about overloading APS with too much work or reporting a case of potential EM that APS eventually determines not to be EM.

6. A video featuring an expert describing how to react to high-risk responses from patients or caregivers. In this context, the term "high-risk" does not necessarily refer to risk to the patient; rather, it refers to the patient and/or caregiver having an extreme reaction when the HBPC clinician informs the patient that they will need to report potential EM to APS. This video aims to address HBPC clinicians' fears about what to do if they ever find themselves in a situation where the patient and/or caregiver has an extreme reaction, including the possibility of danger to themselves.
