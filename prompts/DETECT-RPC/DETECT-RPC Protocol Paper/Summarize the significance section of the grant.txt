"Overview of Elder Mistreatment. Elder mistreatment (EM) is commonly defined as an intentional act, or failure to act, by a caregiver or another person in a relationship involving an expectation of trust, that causes harm or creates a risk of harm to an older adult.1,2 EM takes many forms, including financial abuse/exploitation, neglect, and physical, emotional/psychological, and sexual abuse. Exposure is often chronic and involves polyvictimization.1–3 The prior two decades have seen a sharp uptick in clinical and empirical attention to the prevalence, public health impact, identification, and modification of salient risk factors, as well as strategies for early detection, outreach and intervention efforts to address EM.4–7 Epidemiologic studies estimate that more than 1 in 10 cognitively intact older adults living in the community experience EM annually.8 Risk for mistreatment is higher among older adults with Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias (ADRD), with estimates suggesting an annual prevalence as high as 30-75%.9–11 The public health impact of EM is considerable with EM increasing risk for poor quality of life,12 loss of property and security,12  physical injury,13 depression,14 functional decline,15,16 emergency room visits,17 hospital admissions,18 and all-cause mortality compared to non-mistreated older adults.19–22 The costs associated with lost income, recovery from financial abuse, and the medical, legal and social service intervention needed by mistreated older adults are estimated to be in the billions of dollars each year.2 Nevertheless, EM is difficult to detect and often goes unrecognized. Effective and efficient EM screening tools are urgently needed to improve early detection efforts to preserve family functioning and the health and wellness of older adults and their caregivers. This proposal will address this need by adapting an evidence-based EM screening tool (DETECT) for home-based primary care (HBPC) and rigorously evaluating its potential benefits and harms. 

  EM Detection Challenges. A large body of prior work suggests that elder mistreatment is severely underdetected and underreported, with as many as 80-90% of EM cases never documented.8,23,24 These statistics highlight one of the primary challenges in addressing EM - detection and reporting of older adults living with EM. There are several theories as to why EM is so difficult to detect.  

  First, older adults are often more socially isolated than children and young or mid-life adults.25 Although the majority of older adults (93%) continue to reside in traditional communities,26 they report having about half as many social relationships as younger adults in their 20s or 30s.27 The disintegration of older adults’ social networks includes factors such as exiting the workforce, ailing health and/or function, network members falling out due to disease and death, and deliberate choices to focus on a smaller number of interpersonal relationships.27,28 At the same time, this isolation and lack of social networks are well-established risk factors for EM and can limit opportunities for early detection of EM.8,13,24,29 Despite having comparatively limited opportunities for early detection in general, older adults do encounter their primary care physician 5 times a year, on average, and these interactions may be the only interactions they have with someone other than a family member.30 This underscores the critical role in the early detection of EM that primary care clinicians are uniquely equipped to fill. 

  Second, older adults may have a harder time reporting their own abusive circumstances due to declining health and functional ability. As age increases, so does the prevalence of many chronic conditions and functional loss.31 For example, vision impairments, hearing impairments, and cognitive deficits can affect a person’s ability to report abuse or neglect. Older adults may find that it is difficult to leave the house; further, their physical and cognitive limitations may prohibit (or increase fear of) communication.32 

  Third, older adults may be less likely to disclose that they are experiencing mistreatment, even when they come into contact with the healthcare system.32–35 Some older adults feel responsible for their mistreatment; particularly when the person mistreating them is their adult child.32 In these cases, the older adult may feel ashamed or embarrassed, fear being stigmatized by what has happened to them, or they may fear negative legal consequences for the adult child.32–34 They may also fear retribution or retaliation by the person mistreating them.32–34 In cases where the older adult is dependent on a caregiver, the older adult may fear that disclosing elder mistreatment will leave them institutionalized in a nursing home.32,33 The many barriers to self-report or disclosure of abuse among older adults further highlights the need for the development of an observation-based screening tool that can be used by front-line providers, i.e., primary care clinicians, and DETECT-RPC represents a critical step towards this goal.  

  EM Detection Challenges in ADRD. Detection of EM among people living with ADRD presents several additional challenges to those listed above. First, the clinical presentation of EM can overlap with the clinical presentation of ADRD.36 For example, depression is a commonly occurring feature of both EM and ADRD, but there is currently no existing screening tool that can be used to accurately distinguish whether the depressive symptoms currently being observed by the clinician are due to EM, ADRD, or both. Second, some caregiver behaviors that would be considered mistreatment if done to cognitively intact older adults may be considered acceptable, or even necessary, for the protection of a person living with ADRD or their caregiver. For example, the appropriate use of restraints intended to prevent the person living with ADRD from wandering, falling, or harming themselves or someone else.36 Third, issues of capacity and competency associated with ADRD can cast doubt on an older adult's account of EM occurrence.36 In all of the circumstances outlined above, there is no existing tool or procedure that can be used to reliably provide primary care clinicians with guidance about the potential for EM. DETECT-RPC overcomes these limitations by focusing on observational assessment of validated indicators of EM, thus removing these issues related to ADRD. 

  Universal Screening for EM-Limitations to Current Practice. The past decade has seen a dramatic uptick in the implementation of universal screening for abuse exposure in healthcare settings.37–42 Historically, universal screening for interpersonal abuse exposure has been emphasized in pediatric populations and subgroups of women (i.e., adolescence, and during pregnancy and peripartum periods) who bear a disproportionate burden of risk for abuse exposure.37–42 The need for/value of universal screening for EM has also been widely discussed.42 Guidance for universal screening for EM is limited, however, as the paucity of research on this topic has precluded a concrete recommendation from the United States Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF).42 The work proposed here will help to close this important gap in the clinical literature, providing robust empirical data regarding the feasibility and utility of universal screening for EM among clinicians providing HBPC for more than 2,000 community-dwelling older adults (over a thousand of which are living with ADRD) residing in five geographically diverse metropolitan areas across the United States (i.e., Baltimore, MD; Birmingham, AL; Dallas, TX; Houston, TX; and Palo Alto, CA). Additionally, the proposed strategies for the multi-site recruitment and retention of participants from this diverse set of clinics is intended to maximize representation from underrepresented populations and promote health equity and address health disparities. 

B.2.  EM Screening Tools Designed Around Systematic Observation Methods: A Rationale.     

   The adaptation of a brief screening tool (i.e., DETECT-RPC) that allows primary care clinicians to quickly and systematically surveil an older adult and the older adult’s physical and social environment without the need for direct questioning of the older adult or their caregiver (R61 AIM 1) represent a critical first step towards this goal of universal screening. Efforts to optimize and streamline the DETECT-RPC measure will further facilitate this work, likely increasing the consistency and effectiveness of clinicians’ efforts regarding timely detection of EM. Taken together, these efforts will contribute to greater detection of EM, and foster opportunities for early intervention and compliance with reporting standards and mandates. This work will also help to lay a critical foundation of knowledge needed regarding the impact of universal screening for EM. 

  There is widespread consensus that the field is in need of germane and psychometrically sound detection methods to progress EM research and practice.1,25 The National Research Council, National Institute of Justice, Institute of Medicine, Administration on Aging, and others have all highlighted this gap in our understanding of EM, and have called for more research and for greater resources to be devoted to the detection and prevention of EM.1 The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force issued a call for research into screening older adults and vulnerable adults for abuse and neglect; pointing out that “No [randomized controlled trials] of screening and interventions have been done,” and “studies of most screening instruments are lacking, and most existing instruments remain to be validated.”42 

  The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) highlights several published screening tools for elder mistreatment. However, to our knowledge, none are designed to be implemented efficiently in the context of primary care for community-dwelling older adults.43–50 Almost all existing screening tools rely on direct questioning of the older adult, their caregiver, or both; and, have not been properly validated, or are too lengthy to be used by busy primary care clinicians providing care to older adults in real-world conditions. One possible exception is the Brief Abuse Screen for the Elderly,51 which is a 5-item screening tool that can be completed in less than a minute. BASE has some desirable reliability properties, but this tool has not been psychometrically validated, nor has it been used by primary care clinicians in the community. The BASE is also limited in that the indicators are focused on whether the third-party screener perceives EM to be occurring and urgency of intervention, rather than focusing on the contextual cues and indicators of EM. Our preliminary data suggest that this type of rating system may be problematic, as clinicians hesitate to identify a situation as EM out of fear of “wrecking [an older adult’s] life.”52 Thus, DETECT-RPC not only overcomes issues related to older adult disclosure of EM but also removes the burden of making value-laden judgments from the providers. 

  Unlike hospital or clinic-based providers who have been targets for screening in the past, HBPC clinicians have the benefit of being able to see the older adult where they live. These environmental cues are potentially important predictors of EM that are not visible to the outside world. Several studies evaluating the QUALCARE Scale support this notion; however, the QUALCARE scale requires 1-2 hours to complete and a standardized training for raters rendering it impractical for use in typical HBPC encounters.53–55 Therefore, HBPC clinicians urgently need a valid, reliable, objective, fast, and easy-to-use screening tool based on systematic observation of the older adult and his/her home environment.    

  Rigorous prior work supporting this proposal: DETECT. We previously partnered with MedStar Mobile Healthcare (MedStar) — the largest emergency medical services provider in North Texas — and Texas Adult Protective Services (APS) — the state organization responsible for investigating the abuse, neglect, and exploitation (i.e., mistreatment) of adults who are age 65+ and who live in the community — to develop and test an EM screening tool designed specifically for use by front-line medical providers in older adults’ homes (1R01AG059993-01). The project and screening tool are called Detection of Elder mistreatment Through Emergency Care Technicians (DETECT). The DETECT tool is designed to (1) be brief, (2) be based on the clinician’s direct observations of the older adult and the older adult’s physical and social environment, (3) provide reporting guidance, and (4) be integrated into their existing procedures and medical charting software.56 Our initial results from a 4-year test period in which more than 11,000 older adults were screened indicated that the number of reports to APS increased 3-fold (RR, 3.03; 95% CI, 2.06-4.46) when the DETECT tool was used.57 We expected similarly positive results when the tool is implemented in HBPC through DETECT-RPC. Further, a subset of the DETECT screening items have already been adapted for an emergency department EM screening protocol and implemented in five diverse emergency departments that screened over 15K patients58 - demonstrating the feasibility of adapting DETECT to diverse practice settings. These results provide evidence of our study team’s experience with successfully creating, implementing, adapting, and evaluating EM detection strategies in the community and in complex healthcare environments. In direct response to RFA-AG-22-024, we propose to adapt the DETECT screening tool for HBPC — with special emphasis on older adults who were recently diagnosed with ADRD — The DETECT Revision for Primary Care (DETECT-RPC)."
