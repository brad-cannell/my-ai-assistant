## 🔬 Research & Science

**SK-129 foldamer inhibits alpha-synuclein aggregation across multiple models** | *Science Translational Medicine*, April 2026

Researchers from the Magzoub lab at NYU Abu Dhabi and the Kumar lab at the University of Denver (Knoebel Institute for Healthy Ageing) identified SK-129, a synthetic foldamer — an engineered molecule designed to mimic the folded structure of proteins — that selectively binds to neurotoxic alpha-synuclein oligomers, inhibiting their aggregation and cross-seeding with tau. SK-129 demonstrated efficacy across a range of model systems including human cells, patient-derived tissue, *C. elegans*, and transgenic mice, and critically was shown to cross the blood-brain barrier. In mice engineered to develop PD-like pathology, SK-129 slowed motor deterioration, representing one of the first foldamer-class compounds with potential as a disease-modifying therapy for synucleinopathies. ([Science Translational Medicine](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/scitranslmed.adu1050))

**Repeated donor FMT improves motor symptoms in drug-naïve Parkinson's: Phase 2 RCT** | *Signal Transduction and Targeted Therapy*, 2026

Zhang, Feng, Wang, and colleagues published results of a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled Phase 2 trial of repeated donor fecal microbiota transplantation (dFMT) versus autologous FMT (aFMT) in 72 de novo, drug-naïve PD patients. At 35 weeks, the dFMT group showed significant improvement in motor symptoms (UPDRS III mean change: −3.8 vs. +0.1; p = 0.0001), along with substantially greater reduction in constipation severity — a highly prevalent non-motor symptom and potentially prodromal marker of PD. This is one of the larger and most rigorously designed FMT trials in PD to date, providing substantive clinical evidence for the gut-brain axis as a therapeutic target. ([Signal Transduction and Targeted Therapy](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41392-026-02604-9))

**Blarcamesine rescues motor function and restores dopaminergic nerve fibers in advanced preclinical PD model** | *Anavex / AD/PD 2026 Conference*, March 2026

Anavex Life Sciences presented preclinical data at the AD/PD 2026 Conference demonstrating that blarcamesine — a sigma-1 receptor agonist and candidate disease-modifying agent — produced complete rescue of impaired motor function and biomarker evidence of dopaminergic nerve fiber regrowth in the striatum after just 6 weeks of treatment. The model used, described as an "advanced" alpha-synuclein model, combined alpha-synuclein accumulation with noradrenergic degeneration to more closely replicate PD's dual-pathway pathology. These preclinical findings support blarcamesine's ongoing clinical development program for early PD. ([Anavex / GlobeNewswire](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/03/17/3257034/29248/en/Anavex-Life-Sciences-Presents-Significant-Treatment-Effects-of-Blarcamesine-in-New-Advanced-Alpha-Synuclein-Model-of-Parkinson-s-Disease-at-AD-PD-2026-Conference.html))

**Antiviral innate immunity induces alpha-synuclein phosphorylation independent of aggregation** | *npj Parkinson's Disease*, 2026

A study published in *npj Parkinson's Disease* reports that activation of antiviral innate immune signaling pathways induces phosphorylation of alpha-synuclein at serine-129 (pSer129) — a canonical marker of Lewy body pathology — in neurons even in the absence of protein aggregation. This finding challenges the assumption that pSer129 is exclusively a readout of aggregation and suggests that immune activation may contribute to early PD pathology through a previously unrecognized mechanism. The implications for biomarker interpretation and the link between viral infections and PD risk are significant. ([npj Parkinson's Disease](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41531-026-01297-9))

## 🏥 Clinical & Treatment Updates

**EJS ACT-PD — world's largest PD trial — expanding to full site network through April 2026** | *UCL / Van Andel Institute*

The Edmond J. Safra Accelerating Clinical Trials in Parkinson's Disease (EJS ACT-PD) platform trial, sponsored by University College London and co-funded by the Medical Research Council, NIHR, Cure Parkinson's, The Michael J. Fox Foundation, and Van Andel Institute, is now enrolling at additional sites across England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, with the full network of 40+ hospitals expected to be active by April 2026. The trial uses a multi-arm, multi-stage (MAMS) design to test multiple repurposed drugs in parallel against a shared placebo — beginning with telmisartan (antihypertensive) and terazosin (alpha-blocker), with plans to add ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) later. With 1,600 participants planned in the first phase and £26 million in funding, it represents the largest investment in a disease-modification trial in PD history. ([Van Andel Institute](https://www.vai.org/article/largest-ever-parkinsons-disease-clinical-trial-opens/) | [Cure Parkinson's](https://cureparkinsons.org.uk/2025/10/ejs-act-pd-recruitment-announcement/))

**EPA trichloroethylene (TCE) ban remains in legal limbo** | *Ongoing*

The EPA's December 2024 ban on nearly all uses of TCE — an industrial solvent epidemiologically and mechanistically linked to Parkinson's disease risk — has been stayed pending a legal challenge, leaving the chemical in active commercial use. Advocacy by the Parkinson's community, including over 150,000 petition signatures organized by the Michael J. Fox Foundation, was instrumental in achieving the original ban. ## 💡 Today's Deeper Dive

**FMT Phase 2 RCT: gut-brain axis intervention shows clinically meaningful motor improvement in drug-naïve PD**

The Phase 2 FMT trial published in *Signal Transduction and Targeted Therapy* is among the most methodologically rigorous gut-brain axis interventions in Parkinson's to date and warrants close attention from epidemiologists and clinicians alike. The trial enrolled 72 de novo, drug-naïve patients — a crucial design choice that eliminates confounding from dopaminergic medications — and used repeated colonoscopic donor FMT (versus autologous FMT as placebo) in a fully double-blinded, randomized design. The primary motor outcome (UPDRS III) difference of −3.8 vs. +0.1 at 35 weeks with p = 0.0001 is both statistically and clinically meaningful: a ≥4-point change is generally considered the minimal clinically important difference on this scale. The concurrent improvement in constipation — a well-established prodromal PD symptom that precedes motor onset by years in many patients — raises the hypothesis that gut microbiome modulation may address not only downstream motor symptoms but also a pathogenic mechanism that is upstream in the disease course. From a population-health perspective, if replicated in Phase 3, FMT or microbiome-targeted therapies could represent a scalable, potentially low-cost adjunctive intervention suitable for older adults in early disease stages — a population that disproportionately carries PD burden and is underrepresented in clinical trials. The gut-brain axis also presents a compelling mechanistic bridge to environmental and behavioral risk factors (diet, antibiotic use, gastrointestinal infections) that are amenable to epidemiologic study and, ultimately, prevention.
